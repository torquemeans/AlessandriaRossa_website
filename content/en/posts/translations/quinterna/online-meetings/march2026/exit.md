Tuesday evening's online meeting started with a commentary on the latest developments of the war in Iran.

According to the journalist Federico Fubini, author of the article “[L’Iran e l’Operazione Sansone: giocarsi tutto pur di far saltare l'economia degli Stati del Golfo](https://www.corriere.it/economia/energie/26_marzo_07/operazione-sansone-iran-israele-golfo-prospettive-6e1a8daf-339b-459e-9808-caf8bef4dxlk.shtml)”[^1] published on the *Corriere della Sera*, China might be one of the main beneficiaries of the ongoing conflict. The war and the consequent climate of instability may push Gulf countries to walk out from under the umbrella of the American military, which is regarded as no longer efficient, and to get closer to Beijing.

Currently, Gulf monarchies have to deal with the problem of ammunition supply. The interception of a drone launched by Iran, whose production cost is estimated to be around 20 thousand dollars, generally requires the launch of two or more missiles, whose prices can reach several millions of dollars each. The United States produces 96 interceptor missiles per year, while in only the first week of the war Iran has launched hundreds of vectors. In a few days the missile capabilities have become [saturated](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PMhSIOA7XE), both in defense and in offense. As we wrote in the article “[Teoria e prassi della politiguerra americana](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/11/invasione_iraq.htm)”[^2], modern warfare is highly costly and consumes materiel at a never before seen pace. The serial production of armaments depends on the state of industry, which thus becomes essential. The more the conflict lengthens, the higher the risk that it will become economically unsustainable.

The Iranian bombardment of Israel continues, but  [information](https://www.jpost.com/middle-east/iran-news/article-889169) regarding the damages remains insufficient. According to the reconstruction of some military analysts, the number of devices thrown by Iran is decreasing, while the quality of the missiles being used is increasing. The *wargames* of the USA and Israel are being confronted by Iran’s: in the “Twelve-Day War” everyone was able to probe the strong and weak points of the enemy.

Iran, for example, has applied a strategy known as the [Mosaic](https://www.ilfoglio.it/esteri/2026/03/07/news/cose-la-difesa-a-mosaico-delliran--125783), that is, decentralized Defence. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps has [divided](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c93jj3gz8x0o) the army into 31 autonomous command centres, each one responsible for its own province and ready to act in case of decapitation of the military leadership. Each centre has available to them specific plans and actions to carry out as a response to attacks as part of an overall strategy.

In the war between [machine systems](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/51/la-sindrome-di-yamamoto.htm), radar has a fundamental role, being integrated inside a cybernetic whole that controls departing missiles and intercepts incoming ones. Iran has hit [various](https://www.rid.it/shownews/7897/l-rsquo-iran-colpisce-radar-e-sistemi-di-comunicazione-americani-nel-golfo) radar systems in Kuwait, the United Arab Emirates, and in Jordan, countries where American military bases are present. But the goal isn’t simply militaristic: it is, as a matter of fact, about regionalising the conflict, extending and turning it into a problem from the perspective of global economic and trade management, too. By blocking the flow of oil, Iran indeed puts the capitalist interests of the area and beyond in a difficult spot. According to the *Limes* magazine’s analysis of the conflict, Türkiye might come out of this war on top, precisely thanks to a power projection capable of reaching past the region.

Geopolitics won’t tell us anything unless we also analyse the current mode of production. In *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism*, Lenin describes the transition, between the XIX and XX centuries, from competitive capitalism to monopolistic capitalism, and the ultimate fusion of industrial and commercial capital which gave rise to financial capital, progressively magnified to the point of taking over all of industry. The current world war, still in its primordial phase, unlike the First and the Second, doesn’t plan to conquer new territories, but rather to control flows of value, that is, [ground-]rent. In other words, it is the fight for the appropriation of surplus value produced on a global scale, both through control of the oil flows and through the administration of data which is accumulated and elaborated in *data centres*.

Every State prepares its own *wargames*,  just as every business develops strategies to prevail over their competition. The requisition of oil and raw materials is often pointed out as the goal of the conflict, but it is actually one of the means through which war is fought. Tehran has blocked the Strait of Hormuz, the nerve center of worldwide maritime traffic. The closing of the strait has drastically reduced the flow of crude oil and refined products, which, before the war, amounted to about 20 million barrels a day, equivalent to about 30% of global consumption. Oil is important for the production of certain types of fertilisers, which in turn are essential for modern agriculture. But the war impacts not only this resource: the Gulf region houses some of the largest industrial plants for the production of ammonia and urea in the world, which convert natural gas to nitrogen fertilisers.

Henry Kissinger once said: “Control oil and you control nations, control food and you control the people”. Large cities with 10 or 20 million inhabitants could find themselves without essential goods because of the interruption of supply chains.

Our Planet is made in a certain way and, to allow the transportation of men and semi-finished products, there are obligatory passages. Logistics reveals a system both extremely complex and very fragile: blocking certain nodes can generate a chain reaction. An example is what happened in March 2021, when the Ever Given container ship got stuck in the Suez Canal (Egypt), causing its obstruction.

The “Epic Fury” operation, the military campaign launched by the United States against Iran, should have been a matter of days, but there currently seems to be no end to it and instead we see its repercussions on markets, prices, and raw materials. The *Economist* has titled its March 7th issue “[A war without strategy](https://www.economist.com/weeklyedition/2026-03-07)”. The White House has declared as objectives of the attack regime change, the destruction of Iran’s missile arsenal, and the annihilation of the Iranian nuclear program. But wars “break out” because of automatic processes which no one can stop.

Anthropic is a company famous for developing a family of large language models (LLM) named Claude, which is also employed in the US military. Recently, its collaboration with the Pentagon ended up under the spotlight because the company didn’t allow a completely unconstrained use of its product, in regard to specifically mass surveillance and the implementation of autonomous weapons (without human supervision). In response, the Department of Defense has labeled the company as a “*supply chain risk*”, an unusual designation for an American company, and has [excluded](https://www.ilfattoquotidiano.it/2026/03/04/trump-anthropic-iran-intelligenza-artificiale-news/8311750/) it from public procurement.

According to *The Economist*, in its issue titled “[An AI disaster is getting even closer](https://www.economist.com/briefing/2026/03/05/an-ai-disaster-is-getting-ever-closer)”, there’s a risk that artificial intelligence systems could escape from [human control](https://www.repubblica.it/tecnologia/2026/02/25/news/l_apocalisse_per_andrea_pignataro_l_uomo_piu_ricco_d_italia_insegnamo_alle_ia_come_sostituirci-425183332/), leading to scenarios comparable to an artificial intelligence “Chernobyl”, hence an agreement between China and the United States on global AI *governance* would be desirable. But appeals have little importance: AI is now one of the weapons used in the war and, in fact, the bombings have also involved some *data centres*, the deposits in which are stored enormous quantities of data used by structures such as Amazon and Microsoft.

The journalist Naomi Klein published in 2007 an essay titled *The Shock Doctrine: The Rise of Disaster Capitalism*, to demonstrate how the current economic system cannot help but produce famines, pandemics, wildfires, hurricanes, wars, and crises on which to later speculate on. The Left, sixty years before Klein, wrote a series of articles regarding the capitalist cultivation of catastrophes, collected in the notebook “[Drammi gialli e sinistri della moderna decadenza sociale](https://quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/storici/drammigialli.htm)”[^3], an indirect critique of reformist environmentalism, unable to grasp the root problem of environmental destruction.

“Men started to communicate and write because they needed to coordinate their work” (F. Engels, *The Part played by Labour in the Transition from Ape to Man*, 1876). Writing made it possible to etch information on a physical medium. Today, with the progress of AI systems, another revolutionary leap is happening: [information](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/37/informazione_e_potere.htm) self-organises, making possible a further process of liberation of the human species from the realm of necessity. The capitalists invest in *software* and robotics to take out the competition, but at the same time, fear the consequences of technological development because in it they recognise the crisis of the wage labour system. A contradiction they cannot resolve.
