The Tuesday evening online meeting began with some remarks regarding the recent demonstrations in the United States.

Following the mobilisations of June and October of last year, on Saturday March 28th a new protest under the slogan “[No Kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_2026_No_Kings_protests)” brought over 8 million people into the streets in the United States alone. On the same day, demonstrations with the same slogan took place in Berlin, [Rome](https://www.ansa.it/sito/notizie/cronaca/2026/03/28/no-kings-italy-a-roma-siamo-300-mila-_408cfd0f-0318-4e02-a560-1e6ef583f129.html), London, as well as in Australia and Japan. In Israel, anti-war protests were violently suppressed in Haifa and Tel Aviv. What drives such distant plazas to synchronize?

As we wrote in “[Wargame. Parte seconda](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/51/wargame-parte-seconda.htm)”[^1] the material conditions that drive millions of people to protest, clash with the police, etc. must be sought in the crisis of the law of value. For years, we have observed how the reserveless of the world have at their disposal powerful tools to coordinate – smartphones, social networks, apps – tools that must be understood as productive forces that have broken their firm boundaries and make men spontaneously “organised”.

Beyond the “No Kings” slogan, launched by some “progressive” forces in response to the policies of the Trump administration, the demonstrations had a clear anti-war profile, just as happened last autumn with the Pro-Palestine mobilisations. Millions of people perceive a drastic worsening of their living conditions: war means insecurity, hardship, and a rising cost of living. When masses of “social atoms” move, one cannot reduce the demonstrations to the slogans put forward by the organisers; [our probe](https://www.quinterna.org/archivio/1945_1951/tracciato.htm) allows us to go beyond what individuals, or even movements, say about themselves.

During the last online meeting, we revisited the article “Leggi di simmetria e scenari da incubo”[^2]; in the same issue of the journal (*n*+1, n. 10, 2002), the article “[Imperialismo con l’acqua alla gola](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/10/imperialismo_acquagola.htm)”[^3] was also published, in which we tried to demonstrate how the then Bush administration had put in black and white the American strategy for the years to come:

*“The new [US] doctrine revokes the constitutional prohibition on striking first, erases the previous military model of “containing” the enemy, and contemplates only its destruction. Finally, it tears up half a century of theories on deterrence.”*

The “American *warpolitik*”[^4] against the world did not therefore begin with Trump: at the time of the conflict in Iraq, as well as today in Iran, *“control over the oil flows means control over the flows of surplus value coming from productive sectors and from countries that consume the most energy”*.

*The Economist* has published an article titled “[The nightmare scenario for global trade](https://www.economist.com/interactive/briefing/2026/03/27/the-nightmare-scenario-for-global-trade)”, accompanied by an animated map illustrating logistic routes through the world’s major straits. The model is dynamic and based on the optimal routes, estimating the volume of maritime trade along each path. The map clearly shows that the issue is not limited to the Strait of Hormuz, which has  come into the spotlight due to the war on Iran, but also involves other chokepoints that, in a scenario of global tension, become critical for the functioning of capitalism. The Bosphorus Strait, the Taiwan Strait, the Suez Canal and the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait are equally strategic points. For Europe, for example, a simultaneous closure of Gibraltar and the Suez would represent an economic disaster. At present, the main concerns are the [Strait of Hormuz](https://www.startmag.it/energia/iran-passaggio-stretto-hormuz-yuan-cinesi/) and, to some extent, Bab-el-Mandeb (recently the Houthis launched two missiles toward Israel); but! *The Economist* warns, the situation could worsen.

According to Dan Jørgensen, european commissioner for Energy, after the first month of war in Iran, European governments must prepare to [reduce consumption](https://www.ilsole24ore.com/art/ue-allarme-austerity-energetica-occorre-prepararsi-tempo-AINjUtGC) due to the shortage of certain raw materials: *“Of particular concern in the short term is the EU’s dependence on the Persian Gulf region for refined oil products, exacerbated by the limited availability of alternative suppliers and refining capacity for specific products within the EU”*. The lack of oil and gas is also causing [supply problems](https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/03/19/which-country-is-the-biggest-loser-from-the-energy-shock) in Egypt, Sri Lanka, India and, in general, throughout Southeast Asia. In [Thailand](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/thailand-weighing-cut-oil-tax-energy-prices-soar-official-says-2026-03-26/) (70 million inhabitants) and the Philippines (120 million inhabitants), the gasoline shortage has driven thousands of people to take to the streets: drivers and riders have launched [strikes](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/3/28/philippine-transport-strikers-say-marcos-jr-failing-to-control-oil-prices) and sit-ins.

The war on Iran has been described by the press as a “war for energy”, and indeed energy is one of the weapons through which this global conflict is being fought.

One way to analyze it is the *wargame*: the conflicts in Ukraine, Gaza, Lebanon, and Iran are parts of a single whole, in which each force reacts upon the others. If war reflects society and weapons its industry, today we are faced with a highly automated and potentially out-of-control conflict. From the initial use of drones in Ukraine, we have moved to a “blitzkrieg” with which Hamas managed to bridge the technological gap with Israel, establishing a new symmetry. Leading, with the attack on Iran, to a conflict that sees on one side the use of drone swarms and hypersonic missiles, and on the other the massive deployment of Tomahawk missiles (costing $3.6 million each). The fact remains that war is not won by bombing and technology alone: what remains essential are the grunts.

A second level of analysis, of a more general nature, points the *detector* at the material structure of capitalism. Imperialism is the [last stage of capitalism](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/25/la_crisi_storica.htm), wherein the highest level of socialisation of labour has been achieved. In an ultra-integrated context, where the debt of major countries is skyrocketing, the rate of profit is thinning, and millions of reserveless find themselves with their backs gasping for air, does the current mode of production still have the energy to regenerate itself?

The most realistic scenario appears to be that of a global collapse, with a catastrophic gridlock in the world economy, as Roberto Vacca already observed in his essay *The Coming Dark Age*.

Ever since the post-war period, the USA has been the only country with a power projection capable of waging war on the rest of the world. Today, however, it is beginning to run out of breath and, in an attempt to defend its position as global *rentier*, it triggers [chaotic dynamics](https://www.limesonline.com/rivista/l-america-ingolfa-il-mondo-21388569/?ref=LHHD-R), with potentially catastrophic effects in various areas of the world. The situation in which it finds itself  stems from the development of capitalism on a global scale. Its debt, equal to 130% of GDP, is not its “fault”: the entire world system has sustained this condition, willingly or not, both through savings channeled into large American investment funds (Europe, Japan) and through petrodollars (Gulf countries), i.e., the anchoring of oil to the dollar. Capitalism, understood as a set of social relations, as a transitory mode of production, has generated and conditioned the current structure of the USA, pushing it to undertake actions aimed at protecting its role. Imperialism is gasping for air, and none of the elements within the system will emerge victorious from this historical crisis.

The competition between the United States and China for supremacy in the sector of artificial intelligence makes any control over the development of these technologies difficult. The AI factor will have explosive effects on society, in the short and medium term. Discussions and proposals for a halt on AI exist among industry insiders, aimed at regulating its development, but so far they have produced no significant results... due to the anarchy of the market.

Our guiding school of thought has carried out fundamental work on the historical course of capitalism, identifying a precise trajectory of the latter (“[Un modello dinamico di crisi](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/24/modello_dinamico_01.htm)”[^5]). Just as accelerations occur in the historical crisis of senile capitalism, so too are they observed from the perspective of class struggle. Today, the production of surplus value is becoming increasingly anemic, investments are mostly directed toward financial speculation, and agriculture has almost completely exited the market circuit and is massively supported by state subsidies (“[L’uomo e il lavoro del sole](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/05/lavoro_delsole.htm)”[^6]). In this context, populations become extremely sensitive to price increases in essential goods: if state intervention were to fail in such a strategic sector, revolts could become uncontrollable.
